AI and Australia’s Economy (2027–2030): An Evidence-Based Forecast

1. Thesis

Artificial intelligence is emerging as a general-purpose technology (GPT) with macroeconomic characteristics similar to electrification and the internet. In the Australian context, its primary economic effect is expected to operate through total factor productivity (TFP) growth, driven by the automation and augmentation of cognitive tasks across services, resources, education, and public administration.

The central mechanism is not full job replacement, but task reconfiguration within occupations. This implies short-run labour market disruption concentrated in routine cognitive and administrative work, alongside medium-term productivity gains through complementary capital deepening.

However, the realised economic trajectory between 2027 and 2030 is contingent on three structural variables: the rate of AI capability diffusion, institutional and regulatory responsiveness, and labour market retraining elasticity. These variables define whether AI acts as a productivity multiplier or a displacement shock.

Artificial intelligence is emerging as a general-purpose technology comparable to electricity and the internet. Between 2027 and 2030, Australia is likely to experience a structural productivity shock driven by AI diffusion across services, resources, education, and government sectors.

However, the economic outcome is not deterministic. It depends on three interacting forces: technology capability growth, institutional adaptation, and labour market adjustment speed.

2. Economic Theory Foundation

This analysis is grounded in the neoclassical Solow growth model, where long-run economic growth is driven by technological progress (TFP) rather than capital accumulation alone. In this framework, artificial intelligence functions as a non-rival, scalable technology that shifts the production function upward by reducing the effective labour input required per unit of output.

Complementing this is the task-based model of labour markets (Autor), which reframes technological change not as job elimination, but as task reallocation within occupations. AI disproportionately automates routine cognitive tasks (data processing, basic analysis, administrative coordination) while complementing non-routine analytical, interpersonal, and creative tasks, thereby reshaping occupational composition rather than simply reducing aggregate employment.

Endogenous growth theory further implies that AI adoption may generate feedback loops through innovation spillovers: higher productivity increases investment in R&D and digital capital, accelerating further AI capability development. This creates a potential non-linear growth path where early adoption advantages compound over time.

6. Scenario Analysis

Baseline Scenario (Gradual Diffusion): AI adoption proceeds incrementally across Australian industries, primarily augmenting existing workflows rather than fully automating them. This results in modest total factor productivity (TFP) growth (~1.5% annually), consistent with steady capital deepening and slow institutional adaptation. GDP growth remains stable, and labour displacement is concentrated in routine administrative tasks.

Upside Scenario (AI Productivity Acceleration): Rapid diffusion of generative AI and autonomous systems leads to strong complementarities between labour and capital. Productivity gains accelerate through compounding efficiency improvements in services and knowledge work. This corresponds to the convex growth pattern observed in the GDP projection chart, with potential spillovers into wages and investment rates.

Downside Scenario (Friction and Slow Adoption): Regulatory constraints, low organisational readiness, and trust barriers limit AI integration. As a result, productivity gains are muted and capital investment is inefficiently allocated. Labour displacement risks remain but without offsetting productivity benefits, leading to stagnation in per capita income growth and weaker GDP trajectory.

These scenarios are structurally linked to the GDP and labour exposure models above, where differences in adoption speed and task substitution intensity drive divergence in macroeconomic outcomes.

7. Policy Implications

AI-driven transformation requires a policy response that balances productivity gains with labour market stability and distributional fairness. Policy design must therefore operate across three time horizons: short-term adjustment, medium-term transition, and long-term structural governance.

Short-term (2026–2028): Labour Market Adjustment
Priority should be placed on targeted reskilling programs for occupations with high automation exposure, particularly administrative, retail, and routine finance roles. Income smoothing mechanisms, such as wage insurance or transition support payments, can reduce displacement shocks and maintain consumption stability during structural adjustment.

Medium-term (2028–2030): Productivity Realisation
Policy should shift toward accelerating safe AI adoption through tax incentives for firms investing in productivity-enhancing AI systems. Public investment in digital infrastructure and compute capacity becomes critical to ensure widespread diffusion beyond large firms, preventing productivity concentration.

Long-term: Governance and Distribution
Regulatory frameworks must address market concentration risks arising from AI capital deepening, particularly in cloud infrastructure and foundation model providers. A key trade-off emerges between innovation speed and regulatory oversight: excessive constraints may reduce productivity gains, while insufficient governance may amplify inequality and systemic risk.

Overall, the policy challenge is not preventing AI-driven disruption, but managing its distributional consequences while maximising aggregate productivity gains.

8. Limitations & Uncertainty

Forecasting the economic impact of artificial intelligence involves substantial structural uncertainty due to the non-linear and rapidly evolving nature of AI capabilities. Unlike traditional technological adoption cycles, AI development exhibits discontinuous capability jumps, making extrapolation from historical productivity trends potentially unreliable.

A second limitation arises from measurement constraints. Conventional productivity statistics often lag behind real economic changes, particularly in service-based and knowledge-intensive sectors where AI is most impactful. This creates a time mismatch between observed data and actual structural transformation.

Additionally, adoption is not purely technology-driven but institutionally mediated. Differences in organisational readiness, regulatory environments, and workforce adaptability introduce heterogeneity that cannot be fully captured in aggregate macroeconomic models.

As a result, the scenario outputs in this analysis should be interpreted as conditional trajectories rather than precise forecasts, dependent on assumptions regarding diffusion speed, policy response, and capability growth.
