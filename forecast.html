AI and Australia’s Economy (2027–2030): An Evidence-Based Forecast

1. Thesis

Artificial intelligence is emerging as a general-purpose technology (GPT) with macroeconomic characteristics similar to electrification and the internet. In the Australian context, its primary economic effect is expected to operate through total factor productivity (TFP) growth, driven by the automation and augmentation of cognitive tasks across services, resources, education, and public administration.

The central mechanism is not full job replacement, but task reconfiguration within occupations. This implies short-run labour market disruption concentrated in routine cognitive and administrative work, alongside medium-term productivity gains through complementary capital deepening.

However, the realised economic trajectory between 2027 and 2030 is contingent on three structural variables: the rate of AI capability diffusion, institutional and regulatory responsiveness, and labour market retraining elasticity. These variables define whether AI acts as a productivity multiplier or a displacement shock.

Artificial intelligence is emerging as a general-purpose technology comparable to electricity and the internet. Between 2027 and 2030, Australia is likely to experience a structural productivity shock driven by AI diffusion across services, resources, education, and government sectors.

However, the economic outcome is not deterministic. It depends on three interacting forces: technology capability growth, institutional adaptation, and labour market adjustment speed.

2. Economic Theory Foundation

This analysis is grounded in the neoclassical Solow growth model, where long-run economic growth is driven by technological progress (TFP) rather than capital accumulation alone. In this framework, artificial intelligence functions as a non-rival, scalable technology that shifts the production function upward by reducing the effective labour input required per unit of output.

Complementing this is the task-based model of labour markets (Autor), which reframes technological change not as job elimination, but as task reallocation within occupations. AI disproportionately automates routine cognitive tasks (data processing, basic analysis, administrative coordination) while complementing non-routine analytical, interpersonal, and creative tasks, thereby reshaping occupational composition rather than simply reducing aggregate employment.

Endogenous growth theory further implies that AI adoption may generate feedback loops through innovation spillovers: higher productivity increases investment in R&D and digital capital, accelerating further AI capability development. This creates a potential non-linear growth path where early adoption advantages compound over time.

3. Economic Mechanisms

  • Productivity acceleration via automation of cognitive tasks
  • Capital deepening in compute infrastructure and data centres
  • Labour displacement in routine white-collar tasks
  • Wage polarisation between high-skill and mid-skill workers

6. Scenario Analysis

Baseline Scenario: Moderate AI adoption, gradual productivity gains (~1.5% annual uplift).

Upside Scenario: Rapid diffusion of generative AI and automation systems leads to ~3–4% productivity uplift.

Downside Scenario: Regulatory friction, trust issues, and slow adoption constrain productivity gains below 1%.

7. Policy Implications

  • National AI reskilling and transition programs
  • Tax incentives for productivity-enhancing AI adoption
  • Stronger labour market adjustment policies
  • Regulation balancing innovation and risk management

8. Limitations & Uncertainty

Forecasting AI impacts involves structural uncertainty: model capability growth is nonlinear, adoption is institutionally constrained, and economic measurement of productivity lags real change.
